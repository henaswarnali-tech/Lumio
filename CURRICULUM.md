# LUMIO Curriculum — The Full Picture
### Version 5.0 · With Research Foundations and Assessment Philosophy

## A Letter to Anyone Reading This

This document exists because one person sat down and tried to answer an uncomfortable question: why does every platform that claims to serve the world's most excluded learners still quietly assume those learners have internet access, a functioning school, a stable home, and a brain that works the way the system was built for?

LUMIO started as an answer to that question. This curriculum document is where the thinking behind it lives.

If you're a developer deciding whether to contribute — this will tell you what you'd be building and why it's designed the way it is. If you're an educator who wants to improve a unit — this will give you the research context behind every decision so you can make a better one. If you're a translator bringing LUMIO to a new language community — this will help you understand what's culturally non-negotiable and what's open to local interpretation. If you're a student who somehow stumbled into this file — welcome. You deserve to know exactly what we think you're capable of.

This is not a neutral document. It is built on the belief that the children and adults LUMIO serves are not charity cases or passive recipients of someone else's generosity. They are learners. They are intelligent. They are already doing remarkable things with very little. LUMIO's job is to put tools in their hands and get out of the way.

We're still building this. Some sections need expert review — those are marked. Some decisions we're less certain about than others — those are noted. If you spot something wrong, something missing, or something that could be better: please tell us. See [CONTRIBUTING.md](CONTRIBUTING.md).

## How To Read This Document

This curriculum moves through four stages, and students move through them in sequence. Two moments exist where students make real choices about their direction. We've marked those clearly because they matter — both for how the app is designed and for what it means to a student to finally be asked what *they* want.

```
STAGE 0  →  Placement assessment  →  Student starts at their own level

STAGE 1  →  Foundation Grades 1–5  →  Everyone, together

              ★ CHOICE POINT 1: Which pathway do you want to follow? ★

STAGE 2  →  6 Pathways  →  Technical · Business · Creative · Health · Agriculture · Education

              ★ CHOICE POINT 2: What direction do you want your life to go? ★
                Self-Employment · Job-Seeking · Entrepreneurship

STAGE 3  →  Professional Skill Courses  →  Short, specific, certified

GRADUATION  →  Certificates + Portfolio + Transcript + Ready for what's next
```

## The Research Foundation — What This Curriculum Is Built On

Before we get into the detail, it's worth being honest about where this comes from.

LUMIO's curriculum draws on decades of education research, but it also draws on something research alone can't provide — direct observation of what happens to children and adults when the system fails them, and what they do anyway. We've tried to hold both of those things at once: the rigour of evidence and the wisdom of lived experience.

Here are the foundational ideas that shaped every decision in this document.

**Mastery learning works.** Benjamin Bloom's 1968 research showed that when students are given the time and support they need, the vast majority of them can achieve the same outcomes as the highest performers in a time-limited class. The problem is not the student. The problem is the constraint on time. LUMIO removes that constraint entirely. There are no deadlines, no time pressure, and no limit on retakes. This isn't a concession to low expectations — it's the opposite. It's the structural change that actually produces mastery.

**The "not yet" matters more than you'd think.** Carol Dweck's decades of research on growth mindset demonstrates that how students interpret difficulty determines whether they persist through it. Students who are told they are "wrong" or "failed" tend to disengage. Students who understand that they haven't mastered something *yet* tend to return. Every piece of LUMIO's assessment language is designed around this — not as a feel-good gimmick but as a pedagogical decision grounded in evidence.

**Trauma changes the brain and the classroom.** Bruce Perry and Bessel van der Kolk's research on childhood trauma is unambiguous: a child who has experienced chronic stress, violence, or neglect cannot access the prefrontal cortex — the thinking, reasoning part of the brain — in the same way as a child who feels safe. This has enormous implications for any curriculum targeting vulnerable learners. Teaching methods that create further stress, shame, or anxiety make learning physiologically harder. LUMIO's design — no rankings, no public failure, no time pressure, private emotional processing — isn't softness. It's neuroscience.

**Self-determination drives learning.** Deci and Ryan's Self-Determination Theory identifies three core human needs that predict intrinsic motivation: autonomy (the sense that you are in control of your choices), competence (the sense that you are growing and capable), and relatedness (the sense that you belong to something). LUMIO is designed to satisfy all three. Students choose their own pathway. Mastery-based progression builds genuine competence. The community and shared purpose create belonging.

**The brain can grow.** Michael Merzenich's research on neuroplasticity — the brain's ability to restructure itself in response to learning — is foundational to how LUMIO talks about intelligence with students. Every learner we serve has likely been told, explicitly or implicitly, that they are not smart enough, not fast enough, or simply too far behind. Neuroplasticity research offers a corrective that is not a platitude but a documented biological fact: every time you learn something new, your brain physically changes. You are not fixed. You are in progress.

**Phonics works for literacy.** The evidence base for systematic phonics instruction is among the most robust in all of educational research (National Reading Panel, 2000; UK Rose Review, 2006). LUMIO's early literacy units follow a systematic phonics approach — not because it's fashionable but because decades of comparison studies show it produces better outcomes for more learners, particularly those who don't have strong reading models at home.

**Language acquisition follows patterns.** Stephen Krashen's Input Hypothesis proposes that language is acquired through comprehensible input — exposure to language that is just slightly above the learner's current level. LUMIO's mandatory English thread is structured around this: starting with the alphabet and spoken greetings, moving incrementally through sentences, paragraphs, and eventually professional communication. English is never drilled in isolation from meaning.

**Financial literacy education has evidence behind it.** The OECD's research on financial literacy across 18 countries found that financial education significantly improves financial behaviour when it is taught early, practically, and in ways that connect to students' real lives. LUMIO's financial education starts in Grade 4 and deepens throughout every pathway — not as a business elective but as a fundamental life skill that every student, in every pathway, deserves.

**Creativity can be developed.** The research basis for treating creativity as a learnable skill rather than an innate trait comes from multiple directions — cognitive psychology (Guilford's divergent thinking research), education (Ken Robinson's work on creative education), and economics (NESTA's research on the creative economy). LUMIO teaches specific, practicable creativity techniques across all pathways because the evidence suggests this works.

**Biodiversity loss is accelerating.** The IPBES Global Assessment (2019) found that around one million plant and animal species are currently threatened with extinction — more than at any previous point in human history. This is the scientific context for LUMIO's biodiversity curriculum. It is not environmental sentiment. It is documented fact with direct implications for the food systems, water systems, and economies that LUMIO students live within.

**Climate change is a justice issue, not just a science issue.** The IPCC's Sixth Assessment Report (2022) confirms what climate justice advocates have documented for decades: the communities most severely affected by climate change are predominantly those in low-income countries that have contributed least to global emissions. Bangladesh, to pick one example directly relevant to LUMIO's origin, is among the most climate-vulnerable countries in the world despite producing a small fraction of global emissions. Teaching climate change without teaching climate justice is incomplete education.

**AI is already reshaping the labour market.** The World Economic Forum's Future of Jobs Report (2023) projects that 83 million jobs globally will be displaced by automation in the coming years, while 69 million new roles will be created — a net displacement of 14 million jobs. The roles most at risk are those involving routine data processing, manual labour in structured environments, and repetitive clerical tasks. The roles least at risk are those involving reasoning, creativity, interpersonal relationships, and ethical judgment. LUMIO deliberately builds the second category of skills throughout its entire curriculum.

## Assessment Philosophy — The Full Version

This section deserves more than a paragraph at the end of the document. How we assess students shapes what they believe about themselves. In many cases, it shapes what they are willing to attempt for the rest of their lives.

LUMIO's assessment philosophy is built on six commitments, each grounded in research:

**1. Mastery, not comparison.**
LUMIO never shows students how they compare to other learners. There are no leaderboards, class rankings, or any mechanism that positions one student's success against another's failure. The research on competitive grading (Kohn, 1993; Deci, Koestner & Ryan, 1999) consistently shows that it undermines intrinsic motivation, particularly for learners who are already behind or already feel excluded. Every student's progress is their own story — not a point on a distribution.

**2. Time is not a test.**
There are no timed assessments in LUMIO. The relationship between working quickly and working well is weak. The relationship between anxiety and poor performance is strong — well-documented in test anxiety research going back decades. Removing time pressure costs nothing and removes a barrier that disproportionately affects learners with ADHD, anxiety, trauma histories, and non-standard working memory profiles. These are exactly the learners LUMIO is designed for.

**3. "Not yet" instead of "wrong."**
This is Carol Dweck's contribution, applied consistently and seriously. When a student gets something wrong in LUMIO, the response is never punitive. It is informative: here is what you got right, here is what needs more practice, here is a way to look at it differently. The student can try again after reviewing the relevant content. This is not about protecting feelings — it is about protecting the willingness to try again, which is the actual mechanism of learning.

**4. Mastery is real, not inflated.**
At the same time, certificates in LUMIO mean something. The 70% mastery threshold for grade advancement is deliberate — it represents genuine demonstrated understanding, not participation. Blockchain verification means no certificate can be faked or inflated. We do not pass students through content they have not understood, because that would be a lie, and the students who rely on these certificates to access employment or higher education deserve the truth about their own capabilities.

**5. Progress is visible as movement, not measurement.**
The app shows students their progress as their character moving through a learning map — discovering new territory, reaching new stages, unlocking new choices. This framing is drawn from game design research (McGonigal, 2011; Gee, 2003) on intrinsic motivation and sense of agency. A percentage score tells a student where they stand. A map tells them where they're going.

**6. Effort and accuracy are both worth celebrating.**
LUMIO's feedback system acknowledges both. A student who tries something difficult and gets it wrong after genuine effort receives a different response than a student who skips through without engaging. The research on effort-based praise (Mueller & Dweck, 1998) is clear: praising effort produces more persistence than praising outcomes. This matters especially for learners who have been told — often implicitly, through years of institutional failure — that they are just not capable.

## Core Curriculum Principles

These aren't mission statement words. They're decisions that affect every unit, every lesson, every line of copy in the app.

**Mastery over memorisation.** Students advance by demonstrating genuine understanding, not by spending time or memorising answers. The app tracks conceptual mastery, not time on task. Every assessment has unlimited retakes after reviewing content — because returning to something and trying again is exactly what learning looks like.

**Skills over certificates.** Every lesson is designed around a question: what can the student do differently after this that they couldn't do before? If the answer is "nothing — they just know a fact," the lesson needs to be redesigned. Certificates are evidence of real capability, not attendance records.

**Ethics throughout everything.** Ethics is never a standalone subject in LUMIO. It runs through every pathway, every grade, every career track. Business ethics in the Business pathway. Historical ethics in Grade 4. Medical ethics in the Health pathway. Environmental ethics in Agriculture. Digital ethics in Grade 5. We made this decision because ethical reasoning is a skill, and like all skills, it develops through practice across varied contexts — not through a single class.

**Multiple income thinking from the start.** Teaching students to have one income source is teaching them to be fragile. Nassim Taleb's concept of antifragility — building structures that benefit from variability rather than being destroyed by it — is directly applicable to personal finance. LUMIO teaches multiple income streams explicitly from Grade 4, and deeply throughout every pathway, because financial resilience is not a luxury skill for vulnerable communities. It is a survival skill.

**English alongside everything, not instead of anything.** This decision was made carefully and can be revisited by contributors who have stronger expertise. Our reasoning: English is currently the dominant language of global employment, international higher education, and online economic opportunity. Denying English instruction to learners because they speak another language is a form of gatekeeping, even when it is well-intentioned. At the same time, a student's home language is part of their identity and cognitive foundation — there is strong evidence (Cummins, 2000) that bilingual education produces better outcomes than immersion-only approaches. LUMIO teaches both, simultaneously, from Grade 1.

**Three career directions that are genuinely different.** Most education systems prepare students for one thing: employment. LUMIO prepares students for three: working for themselves, working for others, and building something that works without them. These require genuinely different skills, mindsets, and preparations. Treating them as variations of the same thing would be misleading.

**Creative thinking for everyone.** Creativity is the one skill that consistently appears across lists of what automation cannot replace — and it is the skill most consistently suppressed by traditional education systems (Robinson, 2006; Runco, 2014). LUMIO teaches specific creativity techniques in every pathway and designs simulations that have no single correct answer, because the ability to approach problems with originality is too important to leave only to students who choose the Creative pathway.

**Future-ready, not future-scared.** AI, automation, climate change, and the globalisation of the labour market are not threats LUMIO shields students from. They are realities LUMIO prepares students to navigate. The curriculum is honest about what is changing, honest about what it means, and honest about what students can do about it. Optimism without honesty is not hope — it is deception.

## Neurodivergent Adaptation — Why and How

Around one in seven people worldwide is neurodivergent in some way. In communities with high rates of trauma, malnutrition, and limited access to early childhood support, this proportion may be higher. Any learning platform that doesn't account for neurodivergent learners is, by definition, excluding a significant portion of the people it claims to serve.

LUMIO offers four learner modes, selected during onboarding through eight visual questions that don't require literacy. No diagnosis is needed, and students can change modes at any time.

**ADHD Mode** is built around what we know about attention regulation. ADHD involves genuine differences in dopamine and norepinephrine systems that affect sustained attention, working memory, and impulse control. The mode uses 3-minute micro-sessions instead of longer lessons, provides reward signals every 2 questions rather than at lesson end, reduces visual clutter to a maximum of 3 screen elements, sends break reminders every 15 minutes, and shows progress as character movement rather than abstract metrics. Research on ADHD learning support (Barkley, 2015) consistently finds that frequent reward cycles and reduced cognitive load produce better learning outcomes than extended concentration demands.

**Dyslexia Mode** uses the OpenDyslexic typeface — developed specifically to reduce letter confusion by weighting the lower halves of letters. It increases character and line spacing (which reduces visual crowding), uses a warm cream background (white backgrounds increase visual stress for many dyslexic readers), provides word-by-word audio highlighting during text reading, and offers full audio alternatives for all text content. Dyslexia is not a deficit in intelligence — it is a difference in how the brain processes written symbols, and there are well-documented design interventions that reduce the associated difficulty.

**Low Literacy Mode** is designed for learners who are entering at zero or near-zero literacy. All navigation uses icons. All instructions are audio-first. The interaction pattern is "tap to hear, then tap to respond" rather than read-then-respond. This mode makes the entirety of Grades 1–5 accessible to learners who cannot yet read, so literacy itself is acquired through the curriculum rather than being a prerequisite for it.

**Neurotypical Mode** is the standard experience — full text, standard pacing, visual complexity appropriate for the content.

*What we're still figuring out: we know these four categories are imperfect. Many learners will fall between them or need combinations. We're treating these as a starting point and would welcome contribution from neurodiversity specialists and disability advocates. See [CONTRIBUTING.md](CONTRIBUTING.md).*

## English Communication — Why It Runs Through Everything

We want to be transparent about this decision because it's one that contributors might reasonably push back on.

The argument for mandatory English is pragmatic, not ideological. Research on global labour markets (ILO, 2022) consistently finds that English proficiency is one of the strongest individual predictors of access to higher-wage work in developing economies. Remote work platforms — which represent one of the most significant economic opportunities available to skilled workers in low-income countries — are predominantly English-language environments. International scholarships overwhelmingly require English proficiency. University education at institutions outside a student's home country almost universally requires it.

We are not saying English is superior to other languages. We are saying that in the current structure of the global economy, English proficiency gives LUMIO students access to opportunities they would otherwise be locked out of — and LUMIO exists precisely to unlock those doors.

English is taught alongside the student's home language throughout, following Krashen's comprehensible input theory — beginning with the highly familiar (greetings, numbers, their own name) and building incrementally toward academic and professional fluency. We explicitly do not treat English as a replacement for the student's native language, which is both their cognitive foundation and their cultural identity.

| Grade | English Target |
|-------|---------------|
| Grade 1 | Alphabet, numbers 1–20, basic greetings, colours, shapes |
| Grade 2 | Simple sentences, everyday vocabulary, name and basic information |
| Grade 3 | Short texts, simple paragraphs, questions and answers, email format |
| Grade 4 | Comprehension, structured paragraphs, formal letters, news articles |
| Grade 5 | Academic reading and writing, presentations, professional email, interview basics |
| Pathways | Professional English for chosen field |
| Stage 3 | Industry-specific vocabulary and communication |
| Graduation | CV, cover letter, interview, client communication — full professional level |

## The Three Career Directions — Why We Distinguish Them

This distinction matters more than it might initially seem.

Most education systems — formal and informal — prepare students for a single economic role: employee. You study, you get a job, you work for someone. This is a complete preparation for one life path and almost no preparation for the others. It also happens to be the life path with the most structural disadvantage for people entering from low-income communities, where the barrier to quality employment is both high and unevenly distributed.

Self-employment, job-seeking, and entrepreneurship require genuinely different skills, genuinely different mindsets, and genuinely different risk profiles. Teaching them as three flavours of the same thing would be dishonest and unhelpful.

**Direction 1 — Self-Employment** means you are the business. You trade your skill for money directly. A tailor, a freelance designer, a market trader, a private tutor. Your freedom is real — you set your own hours and your own prices. Your income ceiling is also real — it's limited by the hours in your day. LUMIO teaches this direction honestly: the freedom is genuine, the limitation is genuine, and the path forward involves building multiple income streams over time so your livelihood isn't entirely dependent on your continuing to work every day.

**Direction 2 — Job-Seeking** means entering someone else's system as an employee. This is often the most accessible path immediately after completing LUMIO. It provides stability, structure, and a starting point. LUMIO teaches it without romanticising it — employment has real constraints, worker rights exist and matter, and building additional income streams alongside employment is wise rather than disloyal.

**Direction 3 — Entrepreneurship** means building a system that earns beyond your own hours. It is the highest-risk and highest-potential direction, and LUMIO is honest about both. Most new businesses fail. Most of them fail for the same preventable reasons — no validated market need, insufficient capital, poor financial management. LUMIO teaches students to avoid those reasons rather than to be naive about them.

**Why the combination matters:** The most financially resilient people we know of combine all three — they have employment for stability, freelance work for income diversity, and are slowly building something of their own. LUMIO teaches this combination explicitly, because it's wisdom that doesn't get passed down to people who didn't grow up around financially successful adults, and most LUMIO students didn't.

## Stage 0 — Adaptive Placement

The placement assessment is five minutes of interaction that ensures every student starts at their own level. This matters enormously. A student who enters at Grade 1 and is placed at Grade 1 when they're actually ready for Grade 3 will disengage. A student who is placed at Grade 4 when they're struggling with Grade 2 content will feel immediately overwhelmed. Neither of those students will continue.

The adaptive placement works through five rounds of increasing difficulty. Getting 2/3 correct in a round advances the student to the next level; getting less than 2/3 places them at the entry point of that round's level.

**Round 1 — Absolute beginner:** Tap the cat · Count 3 dots · Find the circle
**Round 2 — Early literacy/numeracy:** Letter recognition · Word matching image · Simple addition
**Round 3 — Grade 2–3 level:** Sentence comprehension · Multiplication · Living vs non-living
**Round 4 — Grade 4–5 level:** Paragraph comprehension · Fractions · Basic geography
**Round 5 — Critical thinking:** Ethical scenario · Percentages · Logical error identification

After placement, the student doesn't see a score or a label. They see their character appear at their starting point on the learning map and the first lesson waiting. No judgment. No comparison. Just a beginning.

*Assessment note: We're aware that placement tests have inherent limitations, particularly for learners whose prior learning has been interrupted or unconventional. The placement is a starting point, not a verdict. Students who feel misplaced can move through content quickly or request review.*

## Stage 1 — Foundation Curriculum
### Grades 1–5 · Every learner · Same journey

Every grade covers literacy, numeracy, English communication, emotional intelligence, body safety, ethics, and appropriate life skills. No grade skips any of these foundations, because they are interdependent — a student who can read but cannot regulate their emotions will struggle to learn in a crisis. A student with strong numeracy but no knowledge of their body's safety signals is vulnerable in ways that affect their ability to engage with anything else.

---

### Grade 1 — The World Around Me

There is something powerful about starting with the student's own world rather than an abstract academic world. The research tradition associated with Paulo Freire — the Brazilian educator who spent decades teaching literacy to marginalised adults — emphasises this: people learn best when the content connects to their lived reality. Grade 1 begins with what every student already has: a body, feelings, a name, a family, a community.

**What students are working toward:** Literacy foundations in their home language · Numbers to 20 · Understanding feelings · Knowledge of body safety · First English words

**Entry:** Zero literacy, any age. There is no minimum age requirement or maximum age limit. An adult entering at Grade 1 receives the same content as a child — designed to be respectful of any age.

**Assessment:** 70% mastery threshold. Unlimited retakes after content review. No time limit. No comparison scores.

#### Unit 1.1 — Sounds and Letters (Home Language)

We start with sounds before symbols because that's how literacy works in the brain. Phonological awareness — the ability to hear and manipulate sounds in spoken language — is the strongest predictor of reading success (National Reading Panel, 2000). Children who can identify that "cat" and "cap" share a beginning sound are primed for reading instruction. Children who haven't developed this awareness yet need it before they can benefit from phonics instruction.

The unit moves from listening → identifying sounds → connecting sounds to letters → blending sounds into words. Every step is practiced before the next is introduced.

Listening to sounds → Vowels and consonants → Letter-sound connections → Blending sounds into simple words → My own name → Letters in the real world → Review game

#### Unit 1.2 — English Begins

Starting English in Grade 1 follows the principle that earlier language exposure, with appropriate support, produces better long-term outcomes (Cummins, 2000). The first English content is deliberately chosen to be highly familiar and immediately usable: greetings, numbers, colours. Students will use these words before the end of the first week.

English alphabet → Numbers 1–10 in English → Basic colours and shapes in English → Simple picture-word matching → My name in English → Hello, goodbye, please, thank you → Greetings and introductions

#### Unit 1.3 — Numbers and Quantity

Early numeracy is built on the concept of quantity before the symbol for it. A child who understands that "five" means *this many objects* can then attach the symbol 5 to a real understanding. Research consistently shows that students who learn number symbols before they have secure quantity concepts develop procedural knowledge that breaks down under pressure (Dehaene, 1997). We start with objects.

Counting real objects 1–5 → Numbers to 10 → Zero → More and less → Number line to 10 → Numbers 11–20 → Simple patterns → Numbers in daily life → Writing numbers

#### Unit 1.4 — My Body, My Feelings, My World

This is where LUMIO departs most visibly from most curricula for early learners. We teach feelings explicitly, from the first grade, because emotional literacy is cognitive literacy — a student who cannot name their emotional state, cannot regulate it, and a student who cannot regulate it cannot learn. This is not a soft-skills detour. It is the foundation that makes everything else possible.

Body parts and their names → Five senses → All feelings are valid — none are wrong → What to do with big feelings → My family — all family types are valid → My community → Living vs non-living → Basic needs: food, water, shelter, safety, love

Here, students also encounter their first ethics lesson — not a lecture, but a question built into the simulations: when you are kind, what happens? When you are unkind, what happens? There is a difference between being polite and being good. Being good sometimes means speaking up even when it's uncomfortable.

**On behaviour with others:** We teach the mechanics of kindness as a practiced skill, not an innate quality. Taking turns. Asking before touching someone's things. Including someone who is left out. This is grounded in the research on prosocial development (Eisenberg, 2000) — young children are developmentally capable of genuine empathy and social cooperation, but they need to be taught how to act on it in real situations.

#### Unit 1.5 — Body Safety and Wellbeing

This is one of the most carefully designed units in the entire curriculum. We've tried to thread a very specific needle: equipping children with knowledge that genuinely protects them without creating fear, shame, or re-traumatisation in children who have already experienced harm.

*Expert review requested: We want this unit reviewed by child psychologists with specific experience in trauma-informed child protection education. If that's you, please read [CHILD_SAFETY_PSYCHOLOGY.md](CHILD_SAFETY_PSYCHOLOGY.md).*

My body belongs to me → Private parts with correct anatomical names → Safe touch and unsafe touch → The right to say no — even to adults → If something feels wrong, that feeling matters → You are never in trouble for telling → Secrets vs surprises → Rest is not laziness → My body tells me things

Why correct anatomical names? Research on child protection consistently finds that children who know the correct names for their body parts are more likely to be understood and believed when they report abuse, and more likely to report it at all. Using euphemisms teaches children that these body parts are shameful, which makes abuse harder to report.

Why "you are never in trouble for telling"? Because most abusive adults explicitly tell children that telling will get them in trouble, or won't be believed, or will hurt the people they love. Naming this manipulation directly and early is protective.

**Simulation — My Neighbourhood:** Students navigate an animated neighbourhood, identify how characters are feeling, and make daily decisions. The simulation has no single correct answer — it shows students that the same situation can be handled in different ways with different outcomes. This kind of open-ended simulation is used throughout LUMIO because research on game-based learning (Gee, 2003) shows that agency and consequence-exploration produce deeper learning than passive instruction.

**Grade 1 Certificate — Blockchain verified on mastery**

### Grade 2 — Reading and Reasoning

By Grade 2, students have the phonological and phonics foundation for reading. The transition from "learning to read" to "reading to learn" is one of the most significant cognitive shifts in early education — and one of the places where under-resourced learners most often fall behind, because they don't have access to the quantity of texts that makes this transition happen naturally. LUMIO tries to close that gap.

**Goal:** Read simple sentences · Add and subtract · Understand health and the body · Community and environment · Deepen emotional and relational skills

#### Unit 2.1 — Reading (Home Language)

CVC words → Word families → Sight words → Simple sentences → Punctuation → My first illustrated story → Reading for meaning

The unit progresses from decoding (what does this say) toward comprehension (what does it mean). These are genuinely different skills that use different parts of the brain, and research shows that students who become fluent decoders but are not explicitly taught comprehension strategies often plateau at Grade 2 reading level without understanding why (Pressley, 2002).

#### Unit 2.2 — English Reading and Writing

English CVC words → English sight words → Simple English sentences → English greetings and introductions → Writing name and basic information → Colours, animals, food in English → Numbers to 100 in English

By Grade 2, students are beginning to build real English vocabulary through the same evidence-based phonics and sight-word approach used for their home language. This parallel structure is deliberate — the brain transfers reading strategies between languages, so strong first-language phonics skills support second-language literacy.

#### Unit 2.3 — Addition and Subtraction

Addition with objects → Facts to 10 → Facts to 20 → Subtraction → Fact families → Word problems → Mental maths strategies

The research on arithmetic fact fluency (Geary, 2004) shows that students who understand the relationships between numbers — rather than memorising isolated facts — generalise their knowledge far more effectively. "Fact families" (understanding that 3 + 4 = 7, 4 + 3 = 7, 7 − 3 = 4, and 7 − 4 = 3 are all the same relationship) is one structural approach to building that relational understanding.

#### Unit 2.4 — Health and Body Science

Why we wash hands → Oral hygiene → Clean water and why it matters → Food groups and what they do → Sleep and the brain → Exercise → Basic care when sick → It is okay to not be okay — mental health is health

This last point deserves attention. For many students, this may be the first time they have encountered the idea that mental health is health — that struggling emotionally is not weakness or moral failure but a normal part of human experience that can be supported. The research on early mental health literacy (Kutcher et al., 2016) shows that when children learn about mental health early and accurately, they are more likely to seek help for themselves and more likely to support others without stigma.

#### Unit 2.5 — Community and Environment

Rules and why communities need them → Fairness and sharing → Respecting difference → The environment and why it matters → Waste and where it goes → Water cycle basics → Where food comes from → Animals in our community

This is where biodiversity enters the curriculum for the first time. Not as an abstract conservation concept, but as something concrete: animals are part of systems that humans depend on. When they disappear from a place, the whole place changes. When you see an insect, a bird, or a small animal, it is part of something larger. This is an age-appropriate introduction to a concept that will develop in depth through Grades 3, 4, and 5 and into the Agriculture pathway.

**On behaviour with peers:** We teach the mechanics of fair and kind social behaviour here — not assuming that children already know them, but recognising that they are learnable skills. Respecting personal space. Not joining in when a group is being unkind. Including people who are left out. Competing fairly — winning without cruelty, losing without resentment.

**Ethics note:** Fairness does not mean everyone gets the same thing. It means everyone gets what they need. These are different, and young children can understand the difference when it's shown to them concretely.

#### Unit 2.6 — EQ, Boundaries, and Mindfulness

Empathy — other people have feelings too → Reading feelings in faces and situations → What helps when someone is sad, and what doesn't → Physical and emotional boundaries → Respecting others' right to say no → Online safety basics → Mindfulness: breathing anchor, 5-4-3-2-1 grounding

The mindfulness content in LUMIO is drawn from evidence-based Mindfulness-Based Stress Reduction (MBSR) research adapted for children (Zoogman et al., 2015). Brief mindfulness practice — particularly the 5-4-3-2-1 grounding technique, which orients students to the present through their senses — has documented benefits for anxiety regulation, attention, and emotional control in school-age children. We include it not as a wellness trend but as a practical, accessible tool.

**Simulation — The Village Challenge:** A community problem with no single solution. Students make decisions about water, waste, and conflict. Different choices produce different outcomes. No single answer is correct. This simulation structure is used repeatedly throughout LUMIO because research on problem-based learning (Hmelo-Silver, 2004) shows that working through ambiguous, real-world-like problems produces more transferable skills than drilling on problems with predetermined answers.

### Grade 3 — Thinking About the World

Grade 3 is where LUMIO students start to think beyond their immediate surroundings — to understand systems, causes, and consequences. This is also where the brain is developmentally ready for abstract reasoning to begin (Piaget's concrete operational stage, roughly ages 7–11), which is why science content like food chains and weather patterns appears here rather than earlier.

**Goal:** Read to learn · Multiply and divide · Natural science including ecosystems · Manage emotions · Brain science basics

#### Unit 3.1 — Reading to Learn (Home Language)

Paragraph comprehension → Main idea and supporting details → Sequence → Cause and effect → Fact vs opinion → Inference → Vocabulary from context → Different text types → First non-fiction article

The shift from decoding to comprehension strategy instruction is one of the most researched areas in literacy education. The evidence (National Reading Panel, 2000; Palincsar & Brown, 1984) clearly supports explicit instruction in comprehension strategies: teaching students to identify main ideas, make inferences, and monitor their own understanding. These are not natural reading behaviours — they are skills that need to be taught directly.

#### Unit 3.2 — English Communication

Reading short English non-fiction → Writing a simple English paragraph → Asking and answering questions → English email format → Maths vocabulary in English → Describing places and people in English

By Grade 3, English is becoming functional. Students can communicate basic needs and information. The introduction of email format here is deliberate — email is one of the primary channels of professional communication that LUMIO students will use to access global opportunities, and learning its conventions early normalises professional communication as a skill, not an elite privilege.

#### Unit 3.3 — Multiplication and Division

Multiplication as repeated addition → Times tables 2, 5, 10 → Times tables 3, 4 → Times tables 6, 7, 8, 9 → Division as fair sharing → Division and multiplication as opposites → Word problems with mixed operations

Times tables are taught in a specific sequence — not alphabetically — because 2, 5, and 10 contain patterns that make them significantly easier to grasp and provide a foundation for understanding the harder tables. This is not a new insight; it's documented in mathematics education research (Anghileri, 2006) and is what good maths teachers have always done.

#### Unit 3.4 — The Natural World and Biodiversity

This unit is doing several things simultaneously, and it's worth explaining each.

Living things and their life processes → Plants: structure, photosynthesis, how they grow → Animal classification → Food chains: what they are, why they matter, what happens when one link breaks → Habitats: what makes a habitat, what destroys one → Water cycle → Weather vs climate — they are not the same thing → Human impact on nature: deforestation, pollution, habitat loss

**On food chains and biodiversity:** A food chain is not just science — it is a survival system. Every species in a chain depends on others. When one disappears, others are affected, and the effects cascade in ways that often aren't visible until significant damage has already been done. The IPBES Global Assessment (2019) found that 75% of the terrestrial environment and about 66% of the marine environment has been significantly altered by human actions. The foods that LUMIO students eat — rice, wheat, fruit, vegetables — depend on insects for pollination, soil organisms for fertility, and birds for pest control. Biodiversity is not an abstract concept. It is the system that produces food.

**On climate:** We introduce climate change in Grade 3 as scientific fact: the Earth's climate is warming faster than it has at any natural point in the last 800,000 years, and the primary driver is human industrial activity. This is not a contested finding — the scientific consensus (97%+ of active climate scientists, per Cook et al., 2013) is unambiguous. We are careful to present this without creating anxiety or hopelessness — Grade 3 is the introduction, not the full weight of the problem. That comes in Grade 4 and 5.

#### Unit 3.5 — Life Skills: Decisions and Problems

What is a decision → Decision-making framework → Peer pressure — what it is and how it works → Conflict resolution 5-step model → Asking for help is a strength → Time management basics → Basic money → Goal setting → Resilience through real stories

**On behaviour with adults:** One of the hardest things to teach, and one of the most important. Respecting an adult is not the same as obeying an adult who is wrong or harmful. Many children — particularly those from communities where authority figures have historically been abusive or corrupt — need explicit permission and tools to distinguish between cooperation that is healthy and compliance that is dangerous. We are not teaching disrespect for adults. We are teaching discernment.

**Ethics note:** Peer pressure. When following the group is wrong. The concept of moral courage — doing the right thing when it is easier, more comfortable, and socially safer to do the wrong thing. This is introduced here and revisited throughout the curriculum because it is one of the most practically important ethical concepts in everyday life.

#### Unit 3.6 — EQ, Brain Science, and Body Safety

Managing anger, disappointment, and jealousy → The pause between feeling and action → Calming strategies → Your brain grows with every new thing you learn → Different types of intelligence — none more valuable → Consent in everyday life, not just in extreme situations → Bystander responsibility → Stress and the circle of control

The neuroplasticity content here is not motivational filler — it is a corrective to something that has very likely been done to every student at this level: they have been told, or allowed to believe, that they are less intelligent than other people. Research by Yeager and Dweck (2012) shows that when students learn that intelligence is not fixed — that the brain grows in response to challenge, that difficulty is a sign that learning is happening rather than that capability is lacking — they demonstrate measurably more persistence on challenging tasks. This is worth teaching explicitly.

**Simulation — The Nature Lab:** Students plant seeds under different conditions, build food chains, track water through the water cycle, and — crucially — simulate what happens when one species disappears from a food chain. This is the kind of systems thinking that takes years to develop through abstract instruction and can be experienced in minutes through a well-designed simulation.

### Grade 4 — The Bigger Picture

Grade 4 is where LUMIO students start grappling with the world as it actually is — including parts of it that are unjust, complicated, or genuinely difficult. This is a deliberate pedagogical choice. Research on critical pedagogy (Freire, 1970; hooks, 1994) shows that learners who are given an honest picture of the world and the tools to think about it are more engaged, more motivated, and ultimately more capable of acting effectively within it than learners who receive a sanitised version.

**Goal:** Write with purpose · Fractions and percentages · World geography and history · Multiple income streams · Climate justice · Relationships and creativity

#### Unit 4.1 — Writing With Purpose (Home Language)

Paragraph structure → Descriptive writing → Narrative → Instructional writing → Persuasive writing → Compare and contrast → Editing → Formal vs informal → My first 150-word structured essay

Writing is thinking made visible. The shift from writing sentences to writing structured essays requires students to organise their own thought — which is a cognitively demanding skill that transfers far beyond writing itself. The research on the relationship between writing and thinking (Emig, 1977; Flower & Hayes, 1981) is consistent: the act of writing clarifies thought rather than just recording it. Students who write regularly think more clearly.

#### Unit 4.2 — English Communication — Intermediate

Writing English paragraphs → Reading English news articles → Formal English letter → English conversation skills → Professional English vocabulary → Describing opinions and arguments in English

By Grade 4, English is approaching functional fluency. Students can read simple news articles, write basic formal communications, and describe their own thinking in English. These are the skills that separate a student who can access global economic opportunities from one who cannot.

#### Unit 4.3 — Fractions, Decimals, Measurement

Fractions visually → Equivalent fractions → Comparing fractions → Adding and subtracting fractions → Decimals → Percentages → Length, weight, volume → Perimeter and area → Real-world word problems

Fractions are notoriously difficult for students taught procedurally without conceptual foundation. Research (Siegler et al., 2012) shows that fraction understanding is one of the strongest predictors of later algebra success. LUMIO starts with visual representations — what does one-third actually look like — before introducing procedures, because the conceptual foundation makes the procedures meaningful and memorable.

#### Unit 4.4 — World Geography, Cultures, and Climate Justice

Physical vs human geography → 7 continents and 5 oceans → How borders were drawn → Climate zones → Natural resources: who has them, who controls them, who benefits → Migration and refugees → Culture as human expression → Human impact at global scale

**On climate justice:** The IPCC AR6 report (2022) documents what climate justice researchers have been saying for decades: the populations experiencing the worst effects of climate change are predominantly those in low-income countries and regions that have contributed least to the emissions driving it. Bangladesh is a specific example relevant to LUMIO's origin — a country that produces approximately 0.4% of global emissions but has been consistently identified as one of the most climate-vulnerable nations on Earth. Teaching climate change without teaching this asymmetry is incomplete.

We are careful here to distinguish between what is documented scientific fact and what involves value judgments about responsibility and response. Students are taught to make this distinction themselves — a critical thinking skill that applies far beyond climate.

**Action without despair:** We explicitly pair every lesson on a climate problem with documented actions — at individual, community, and systemic level — that students can engage with regardless of their circumstances. The research on climate anxiety (Clayton, 2020) shows that information about climate change without accompanying sense of agency produces anxiety and disengagement. Information paired with meaningful action possibilities produces engagement and motivation.

#### Unit 4.5 — History Through People

What is history and why it matters → Ancient civilisations → How knowledge spread between cultures — often in directions opposite to what standard textbooks suggest → Colonisation — honest, age-appropriate, non-traumatising → Resistance and independence → The 20th century → Local history in each language version → Ordinary people who changed history → You are making history now

**On honest history:** We have made a deliberate choice to present history accurately rather than comfortably. Colonisation happened. It had devastating consequences for colonised peoples. Many contemporary inequalities — including the ones affecting LUMIO students — are direct descendants of those systems. Teaching this is not politically divisive — it is factually accurate, and it is directly relevant to the lives of the students LUMIO serves.

At the same time, we are careful about how this is taught. The goal is not to produce anger or helplessness but understanding — and to show that resistance and agency have always existed alongside oppression.

#### Unit 4.6 — Money, Multiple Income Streams, and Independence

This is where financial education shifts from awareness to depth. The OECD's research (2020) on financial literacy education finds that it is most effective when taught early, practically, and in connection to students' actual circumstances — not through abstract scenarios involving families with disposable income.

What money actually is → How value is created → Active income: trading time for money → Passive income: systems that earn for you → Why one income source is one point of failure → Real examples of multiple income streams in everyday life → Budgeting → Saving as non-negotiable → Wants vs needs → Introduction to entrepreneurship

**On the passive income claim:** We are explicit with students that passive income is not a shortcut. It requires real upfront work to build. The "passive income" content that circulates on social media is often misleading, occasionally fraudulent, and targets people in exactly the financial circumstances of many LUMIO students. LUMIO teaches the real path: build one income source first, stabilise it, then use a portion of your time and income to build a second. This is how financial resilience is actually built — slowly, with consistency.

**Creativity as economic skill:** The introduction of creativity as an economic tool rather than just an artistic one is deliberate. Research from the NESTA creative economy work (2013) and the World Economic Forum's Future of Jobs framework consistently places creative problem-solving among the skills most resistant to automation. This is the first mention of that connection. It will deepen through Grade 5 and every pathway.

#### Unit 4.7 — Relationships, Animals, and Inner Strength

Healthy vs unhealthy friendships → Relationships that build you up vs drain you → Behaviour with animals and why it matters → Self-compassion → Gratitude without toxic positivity → My support network → Working memory and attention

**On animals:** Every creature capable of experiencing pain deserves not to have pain inflicted on them unnecessarily. This is not a sentimental position — it is the basis of animal welfare law in most countries, and it is supported by substantial scientific evidence of sentient experience in mammals, birds, and other species (Marian Stamp Dawkins, 2012). We also note that cruelty to animals is a recognised indicator in developmental psychology of other serious problems. This is taught matter-of-factly and without shame — most students who have been unkind to animals have not been taught why this is wrong. We are teaching them now.

**On respecting difference:** People of different ages, abilities, religions, and backgrounds deserve the same basic dignity. Curiosity about difference is healthy. Mockery of difference is not. This distinction — between being interested in someone different from you and making them feel less than human for that difference — is one of the most practically important social skills we teach.

**Simulation — The Community Garden:** Multi-variable problem combining measurement, budgeting, community decision-making, and managing disagreement. Multiple solutions. No single correct answer. Students discover that the same constraints can be navigated in genuinely different ways.

### Grade 5 — Thinking Critically, Preparing to Choose

Grade 5 is designed to give students the tools to make an informed choice about their pathway — and to give them a genuine picture of the world they are entering. This includes the difficult parts: AI replacing jobs, a destabilising climate, persistent economic inequality. It also includes the genuinely exciting parts: a global labour market that has never been more accessible to skilled people in low-income countries, an internet full of tools and knowledge that used to cost a fortune, and a generation of people who grew up with nothing and built something anyway.

**Goal:** Critical thinking · Full economics · Digital literacy and AI · Self-knowledge · Pathway and career direction exploration · University and scholarship awareness

**Exits to: ★ CHOICE POINT 1 — Pathway selection ★**

#### Unit 5.1 — Critical Thinking

What an argument is: claim, evidence, reasoning → Strong vs weak evidence → Logical fallacies — how smart people are tricked → Bias — everyone has it, including you → Media literacy → Statistical literacy: what numbers actually mean → Ethical reasoning applied to real situations → Structured debate → My 200-word argument on a real issue

The case for explicit critical thinking instruction is well-documented (Abrami et al., 2008). Students who receive explicit instruction in argumentation, evidence evaluation, and logical reasoning perform better on a wide range of academic and real-world tasks than students who acquire these skills implicitly, if they acquire them at all. Given that LUMIO students are likely entering a media environment full of misinformation, political manipulation, and commercial persuasion, critical thinking is not a luxury subject. It is self-protection.

#### Unit 5.2 — English Communication — Pre-Professional

Reading academic English → Academic English writing → English presentation skills → Professional email → Interview basics → Describing skills and experience in English

By Grade 5, English communication is approaching the level required for international scholarship applications. This is intentional — students who complete Grade 5 should have the English foundation needed to continue developing toward scholarship and university application readiness.

#### Unit 5.3 — Economics and Financial Independence

What is an economy → Supply and demand → How money moves → Income types: salary, freelance, business profit, passive, investment → Building multiple income streams step by step → Budgeting at adult level → Debt: tool or trap → Entrepreneurship: from idea to first paying customer → Global economy and trade → Economic inequality: causes and what the evidence says actually changes it → Financial goal setting for life

**On the gig economy and remote work:** The emergence of global remote work platforms represents, for skilled workers in low-income countries, one of the most significant economic opportunities in the history of global labour. A developer in Dhaka can earn rates that were previously available only to people in London or New York. The barriers to entry — language, professional skills, a digital presence — are exactly what LUMIO builds.

**On careers in a changing economy:** This is where LUMIO is most explicit about automation. The WEF Future of Jobs Report (2023) projects net job displacement of 14 million roles over the next five years. The jobs most at risk are those involving routine data entry, basic customer interactions, and predictable physical tasks. The jobs most protected are those requiring judgment, creativity, interpersonal skill, and ethical reasoning. We teach students directly: here is what is changing, here is what is being replaced, here are the skills LUMIO has been building throughout your time with us that are genuinely resistant to automation.

#### Unit 5.4 — Digital Literacy, AI, and the Future

How the internet works → Effective and critical searching → Source evaluation → Online safety → Professional digital communication → Social media: real benefits and documented harms → Your digital footprint → Artificial intelligence: what it actually is

**AI literacy in depth:** AI is one of the most misunderstood technologies in the public conversation, and the gap between what AI is and what people think it is causes real problems — both for people who are unnecessarily afraid of it and for people who trust it uncritically. LUMIO teaches what AI actually is: a pattern-matching system trained on large quantities of human-generated data. It produces outputs that look intelligent because it has been trained on millions of examples of intelligent outputs. It is not conscious. It does not understand. It can be confidently wrong.

This matters because: (1) it helps students use AI tools effectively, (2) it helps students catch AI errors rather than propagating them, and (3) it helps students understand which jobs AI will and won't replace — allowing them to invest their learning time wisely.

**AI can do:** Generate text, images, code, audio · Translate languages · Summarise documents · Identify patterns in data · Automate repetitive tasks

**AI cannot reliably do:** Reason ethically · Make genuinely original creative decisions · Build human relationships · Work correctly without verification · Understand context in the way humans do

**AI ethics:** Who owns AI-generated content (this is currently unresolved in law) · Bias in AI systems and how it got there · The environmental cost of large AI systems · The responsibility of people who use AI toward people affected by it

*Research base: UNESCO AI Competency Framework for Students (2023); MIT Media Lab AI literacy research; OECD AI Principles (2019)*

#### Unit 5.5 — Self-Knowledge and Pathway Choice

Multiple intelligences: no single type of smart → My learning style → My values → My strengths → Things I find naturally easy → Metacognition: thinking about my own thinking → All 6 pathways through real people's stories → Self-employment vs job-seeking vs entrepreneurship: honest comparison → You can choose one or two pathways → You can change direction later

**On multiple intelligences:** We draw on Gardner's framework here while being transparent that it is contested in cognitive science — the evidence for distinct neural systems corresponding to each "intelligence" is weak. What is well-supported is the broader claim: different people have different cognitive profiles, and traditional academic measures capture only a subset of meaningful human capability. The practical value of the framework for our students is not its precise neuroscience but its cultural message: there are many ways to be capable, and the one the school system measured you on is not the only one that matters.

**Creativity as a life skill — in depth:** Creativity is not talent. It is the skill of making unexpected connections between existing ideas to produce something new. Every human brain can do this. Most formal education systematically suppresses it by rewarding only predetermined correct answers (Robinson, 2006). LUMIO's commitment to simulations with multiple correct answers, and to explicitly teaching creativity techniques, is a structural attempt to reverse this suppression. The specific techniques taught — brainstorming, mind mapping, lateral thinking, random stimulus methods, creative constraint — come from cognitive psychology research on divergent thinking (Guilford, 1967) and from design education practice.

#### Unit 5.6 — Mental Health, Rights, and Advocacy

What mental health is → Anxiety, low mood, grief: normalised and explained → When professional support helps → Supporting a friend without burning yourself out → Child and human rights: UNCRC simplified and applied → Standing up for yourself with evidence and calm → Standing up for others → Advocacy: how change happens from the community level

The UN Convention on the Rights of the Child is international law that most countries have ratified. Most children covered by it have never heard of it. Knowing your rights is the first step to claiming them. LUMIO teaches the UNCRC in simplified, applied form — not as an abstract legal document but as a set of practical entitlements that apply to every student's life.

#### Unit 5.7 — University and Scholarship Readiness (Optional)

What university actually requires → Academic writing structure → Research and referencing → How to write a personal statement — your story, honestly told → How LUMIO transcripts and portfolios support applications → What to expect in university's first year → Scholarship basics: what they are, where to find them, what they require → Choosing whether university is right for you — it is not the only path

*This module is optional but strongly recommended for any student who wants to pursue higher education.*

**Simulation — The Debate Chamber:** Real-world issue. Research sources. Build an argument. Present it. Respond to challenges. Revise your position based on new information. The simulation is specifically designed to produce the experience of changing your mind based on evidence — which is one of the hardest and most important intellectual skills LUMIO teaches.

**Simulation — The Business Simulator:** Start with small capital. Build a micro-business through 10 simulated months. Face real challenges: a competitor who undercuts you, an economic downturn, a product that doesn't sell. Adapt strategy. Discover whether you enjoy this kind of thinking — because that discovery is part of making an informed pathway choice.

### 🔧 Technical Pathway

**Level 1 — How Technology Works**

Before writing a single line of code, students understand what code actually does. Research on computing education (Lye & Koh, 2014) consistently finds that students who understand the conceptual foundations of computing — how computers process instructions, what data actually is, how networks function — write better code and debug more effectively than students who are taught syntax first.

How computers process instructions → Hardware and software: what does what → How the internet actually works → Binary and data basics → History of technology — including who built what and who is often left out of that history → Why open source matters and how to contribute

**Ethics:** Who owns technology? Who benefits and who is excluded? The digital divide is not a natural phenomenon — it is a consequence of decisions made by people with power. Students who build technology have a responsibility to consider who they are building it for and who they might be excluding.

**Multiple income streams in technical work:** Freelance development · Teaching technical skills · Building digital products that earn passively · System maintenance contracts · Remote work for international clients at international rates

**AI in technical work:** How AI tools assist developers today · The difference between AI-assisted development and AI-replaced development · Using AI code assistants responsibly, including verification of AI-generated code · Which parts of technical work remain most human

**Level 2 — Logical Thinking and Problem Solving**

Algorithmic thinking → Flowcharts → Breaking complex problems into steps → Debugging: finding and fixing errors → Logical operators → Introduction to data and how it is stored

*Research note: Problem decomposition — the skill of breaking a complex problem into manageable parts — is one of the most transferable cognitive skills available. It applies to technical problems, business problems, health emergencies, and life decisions. Teaching it through technical contexts is effective because the technical context provides clear, unambiguous feedback: either the flowchart works or it doesn't.*

**Simulation — The Bug Hunt:** An animated factory is failing. Something in the logic is wrong. Students work through the flowcharts to find and fix the errors. The simulation is specifically designed to make debugging feel like detective work, not punishment — because the emotional relationship with errors is as important to develop as the technical skill.

**Level 3 — First Code**

Scratch visual programming → Python fundamentals: variables, loops, conditions, functions → Every concept built into something that runs and does something real → Code review and documentation basics

*Research note: The sequence from Scratch to Python follows a well-established progression in computing education. Scratch provides the conceptual experience of programming logic without the cognitive load of syntax. Python then introduces syntax in a context where the underlying logic is already understood. Students who learn syntax-first without conceptual grounding consistently hit a wall when problems become more complex.*

**Simulation — Build a Game:** Across 10 lessons, students build a working game — one feature per lesson. By the end, they have built something real that they can show to people. This matters. Research on project-based learning (Thomas, 2000) shows that learners are significantly more motivated and retain more when they are building something real for a real audience.

**Level 4 — Web and Applications**

HTML structure → CSS styling → JavaScript behaviour → What no-code tools can and cannot do → Building a real webpage that works on mobile → API basics: how applications talk to each other → What makes an app actually function

**Simulation — Launch Day:** Students design, build, test, and deploy a real working webpage for a real community organisation. This is not a mock project. The organisation is real. The deployment is real. The feedback from users is real.

**Level 5 — Building Real Things**

First complete real project from brief to deployment → Database concepts → User experience design basics → Testing with real users → Documentation → Open source contribution: your first pull request

### 💼 Business Pathway

**Level 1 — Money and Mind**

We start with the psychology of money rather than the mechanics, because the research on financial behaviour (Thaler & Sunstein, 2008; Kahneman, 2011) is unambiguous: people's financial decisions are predominantly driven by psychological factors, not rational calculation. Understanding why we make the financial decisions we do — including many that we know are poor decisions — is the foundation for being able to make different ones.

Financial psychology: why people make poor financial decisions even when they know better → Personal budgeting: the system that actually works → Systematic saving as a non-negotiable habit → Understanding debt: the mechanics, the psychology, the trap → Credit: how it works and when it helps → Financial goal setting for a lifetime

**Simulation — Your Financial Life:** 12 simulated months of personal finance management with realistic income variability and unexpected expenses. Students experience the consequences of different financial decisions in a context where the consequences are emotionally real but practically reversible — the ideal learning environment for financial behaviour change.

**Level 2 — Finding the Opportunity**

Identifying real problems worth solving — vs problems that only seem important to you → Market research without spending money → Idea validation: asking real people before building anything → Understanding what customers actually want vs what they say they want → Minimum viable product: the smallest version that tests your idea → Why most businesses fail before they start — the real reasons, not the mythology

*Research note: The failure rate for new businesses is routinely cited at 90% over five years, but the causes are well-documented and largely preventable: no validated market need (the most common), insufficient capital, the wrong team, and outcompeted products. LUMIO teaches students to address the first of these — which is responsible for more failures than all others combined — through rigorous validation before commitment.*

**Ethics:** The difference between identifying an opportunity and exploiting a vulnerability. The difference between solving a real problem and manufacturing a fake problem so you can sell a solution to it. These distinctions matter, and they are not always obvious. Ethical marketing: what forms of persuasion are legitimate and what cross into manipulation.

**Multiple income streams:** How business owners build income beyond their primary product — licensing their method to others, creating digital products, teaching others what they know, consulting, affiliate relationships, building assets that earn while they work on other things.

**AI in business:** The AI tools that are already changing business practice — customer service automation, marketing copy generation, bookkeeping assistance, research acceleration. The important distinction between AI that frees your time for higher-value work and AI that simply reduces quality while reducing cost.

**Level 3 — Building the Business**

Legal registration basics — localised per language version → Pricing strategy: how to charge what your work is actually worth, not what you think people will pay → Finding first customers without a marketing budget → Sales without manipulation → Customer service as competitive advantage → Basic bookkeeping: the numbers that actually determine whether your business is viable → When to spend and when to save

**Simulation — Year One:** Students run a business through its first year — monthly financial decisions, customer decisions, unexpected challenges. The simulation is deliberately not optimistic. Not all decision paths lead to success. Experiencing a simulated business failure in a context where the consequences are educational rather than catastrophic is one of the best ways to learn what failure actually teaches, without the cost.

**Level 4 — Digital Business and Multiple Incomes**

Online selling platforms → Digital marketing fundamentals → Email marketing: consistently the highest-ROI digital marketing channel → Social media for business: what actually produces results and what consumes time without producing them → Free tools to run a lean business → Invoicing and receiving international payments → Passive income through digital products → Building a second income stream

*Research note: The evidence on email marketing consistently shows return on investment significantly higher than social media advertising for most small businesses. Yet most small business content focuses on social media. We teach what the evidence supports, not what is currently fashionable.*

**Level 5 — Growth and Long-Term Sustainability**

When and how to hire the first person — one of the most consequential business decisions → Managing cash flow at scale: more businesses are destroyed by cash flow problems than by strategic failure → Reinvestment decisions: when to grow and when to consolidate → Building systems that work when you are not there → Long-term business planning → Exit strategies → Community responsibility as a business grows: the ethics of success

### 🌱 Agriculture Pathway

**Level 1 — Soil, Seeds, and Systems**

Agriculture is one of the oldest and most sophisticated knowledge systems humans have developed. ## Child Safety and Trauma-Informed Design

We want to explain our thinking here in some detail, because these decisions are the ones where getting it wrong causes the most harm.

**The core principle:** Teach what a child can do. Never frame content around what has been done to them.

Trauma-informed practice (Perry, 2006; van der Kolk, 2014) starts from the understanding that many children have experienced events that affect their nervous system's basic regulatory functioning. A child who has experienced chronic stress or abuse does not process information, build relationships, or respond to authority in the same way as a child who has not. Content that re-activates trauma responses — through shame, graphic descriptions, or forced disclosure — does not help these children. It re-harms them.

**The trust ladder:** Many educational resources tell children to "tell a trusted adult" if something is wrong. This advice assumes the existence of a trusted adult — which many LUMIO users simply do not have. Their parents or guardians may be the source of the problem. The teachers at their school, if they have one, may not be trained to respond or may be legally required to report in ways that create additional danger for the child. "Tell a trusted adult" is not wrong advice — but as the only advice, it fails the children who need help most.

LUMIO teaches a trust ladder instead:
1. Name it to yourself — your feelings are valid information, not evidence that something is wrong with you
2. Write it in your private journal — even if no one ever reads it
3. Tell one person outside your immediate situation if you can safely do so
4. Access a helpline if one exists and is safe in your location
5. Keep this knowledge — use it when you are older and in a safer situation

**Private journal:** After sensitive lessons, LUMIO offers a private journal stored only on the device. Never synced. Never accessible to anyone else. One gentle offer. No pressure. No consequences for using it or not using it.

**No AI for sensitive disclosures.** LUMIO does not use AI chat to respond to trauma disclosures, abuse reports, or statements of crisis. We made this decision because: (1) AI responses in these contexts can be harmful, unpredictable, or legally complex; (2) children who disclose to AI may believe they have been helped when they have not; (3) the appropriate response to a child disclosing harm is human connection and appropriate referral, neither of which AI can provide. This decision is permanent and changes only with formal involvement from qualified trauma specialists.

Full guidelines and open invitation to child psychologists and trauma specialists: [CHILD_SAFETY_PSYCHOLOGY.md](CHILD_SAFETY_PSYCHOLOGY.md)

## Assessment Philosophy — Applied Throughout

We put a long version of this earlier in the document. Here is the practical version: how each principle appears in the actual experience of using LUMIO.

**"Not yet" never "wrong."** When a student gets something wrong, LUMIO shows them what they got right, identifies the specific concept that needs more practice, offers a different way to look at it, and invites them to try again after reviewing. The word "wrong" never appears. The word "failed" never appears. This is not euphemism — it is accurate. A student who hasn't mastered something yet is not wrong. They are in progress.

**Unlimited retakes, always after review.** A student cannot simply click through an assessment repeatedly. After an unsuccessful attempt, the relevant lesson content reappears with an invitation to review before trying again. This is Bloom's mastery learning made concrete: the assumption that every student can achieve mastery given enough time and appropriate support.

**No scores, only movement.** The app shows students their character moving through a learning map. New territory opens up. New choices become available. Progress is spatial and narrative, not numerical. There is no percentage, no grade letter, no points total. The research on self-determination theory (Deci & Ryan, 2000) shows that external rewards — including grades — undermine intrinsic motivation when people are already intrinsically engaged. LUMIO tries to maintain intrinsic motivation rather than replacing it with external metrics.

**Certificates mean something.** The 70% mastery threshold is not arbitrary. It represents genuine demonstrated understanding — not perfect knowledge, but real capability. Blockchain verification means these certificates cannot be inflated. We respect students enough to make the certificates worth having.

**Effort celebrated alongside accuracy.** The feedback system distinguishes between a student who tried something difficult and didn't get it versus one who skipped through without engaging. Both receive support, but the student who showed genuine effort receives specific recognition of that. Research on effort-based praise (Mueller & Dweck, 1998) shows that this is more motivating and produces more persistent learning than outcome-based praise.

## Offline Architecture

Each grade downloads as approximately 60–90MB including audio, animations, and simulations. Every lesson, simulation, and assessment works fully offline. Progress syncs and certificates issue when internet reconnects.

The offline-first commitment comes from the communities LUMIO is designed for. Many of them have intermittent, expensive, or no internet access. A learning platform that requires connectivity is a learning platform that excludes them. LUMIO's content is downloaded in chunks — one grade at a time, approximately the size of a small music album. Students can learn for weeks or months between internet connections.

## A Note on What We Got Wrong

Every curriculum document is also a record of decisions that might be revisited. Here are the ones we're least certain about, where we most want expert input:

The neurodivergent modes are a starting point. The four categories (ADHD, dyslexia, low literacy, neurotypical) don't capture the full range of how different learners experience different content. We need neurodiversity specialists to review and improve this.

The child safety unit has been designed with care but not with clinical expertise. We need child psychologists — specifically those with experience in trauma-informed education — to review Grade 1 Unit 1.5 and the trust ladder approach in detail.

The first aid content has been designed to align with International Red Cross standards but has not yet been reviewed by qualified first aid instructors. This needs to happen before this content is deployed.

The history content varies in quality across different language versions. The version of colonisation history taught in a curriculum used in Bangladesh will be different from the version taught in a curriculum used in Kenya or Mexico. Contributors from each language community need to review and adapt this content to be locally accurate and appropriate.

We are building something that affects real people, including some of the most vulnerable people in the world. We would rather acknowledge uncertainty than project false confidence.
